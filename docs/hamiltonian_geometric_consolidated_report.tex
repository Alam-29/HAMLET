\begin{abstract}
This manuscript develops and evaluates a Hamiltonian--geometric optimizer for
machine learning. It presents (1) the mathematical background of the
framework; (2) an analytical validation of the update equations against the
underlying Hamiltonian mechanics; and (3) empirical evidence from classical,
geometric, and variational quantum benchmarks, together with visual
diagnostics of the metric structure. A dedicated
derivation shows how gradient descent, momentum, Adam/RMSProp-style diagonal
preconditioning, natural-gradient-style methods, entropy descent, and the full
Hamiltonian-geometric optimizer arise within a single update family; it also gives
Adam's diagonal-metric update from a local variational step principle, and a
further derivation extends the same update family to modern optimizers
(AdamW, Lion, Shampoo/K-FAC, and Muon), each obtained by a specific choice of
metric, kinetic term, or potential splitting rather than by analogy. The
analysis also establishes the sign convention for the spectral Hamiltonian and
supplies an explicit vector-to-matrix construction for the memory metric
$M_{ij}$, yielding a self-contained formulation suitable for empirical
comparison.
\end{abstract}
\vspace{0.35em}
\begin{center}
\small\textbf{Keywords:} Hamiltonian optimization; Riemannian metrics;
adaptive optimizers; geometric machine learning; variational quantum algorithms.
\end{center}

\section{Introduction}

Optimization algorithms in machine learning can often be interpreted as
discretized dynamical systems on parameter space. Gradient descent is the
overdamped limit of a flat dissipative flow; momentum methods retain an
inertial state; adaptive methods introduce coordinate-dependent scaling; and
natural-gradient methods replace Euclidean geometry by a task-dependent
metric. These viewpoints are usually presented separately, which obscures the
common geometric structure shared by many optimizer families.

The Hamiltonian--geometric framework studied here treats parameters as
generalized coordinates, the loss as potential energy, and the optimizer state
as momentum evolving on a metric parameter manifold. This formulation gives a
single update law containing flat-gradient methods, momentum, diagonal
adaptive preconditioning, natural-gradient-style updates, entropy descent, and
the full Hamiltonian-geometric optimizer as limiting or structured cases. The
central question is therefore twofold: whether the formulation is internally
consistent as a mathematical dynamical system, and whether the additional
geometric structure yields measurable advantages on workloads where curvature,
conditioning, or phase-space behavior are relevant.

The manuscript makes three contributions. First, it derives the unified update
family and gives a variational derivation of Adam as a diagonal-metric local
step. Second, it extends the same view to modern optimizers, including AdamW,
Lion, Shampoo/K-FAC, AdaFactor, LARS/LAMB, and Muon. Third, it evaluates the
method on classical learning tasks, physics-informed learning, geometric
diagnostics, a chaotic kicked-top control problem, and QAOA MaxCut. The
resulting evidence supports a bounded claim: Hamiltonian-geometric
optimization is especially effective when full-metric structure is available,
while remaining competitive rather than uniformly dominant against the
strongest modern baselines.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

The dynamical-systems view of optimization is not new, and the contribution
here must be positioned against it precisely rather than implied to be
novel by omission. Su, Boyd \& Cand\`es~\cite{su2016differential} first
modeled Nesterov's accelerated gradient method as the small-step-size limit
of a second-order ODE, opening a line of work that reads discrete optimizers
as discretizations of continuous-time dynamics.
Wibisono, Wilson \& Jordan~\cite{wibisono2016variational} generalized this to
a family of Bregman-Lagrangian dynamics whose Euler--Lagrange equations
recover essentially all known accelerated first-order methods, already
placing momentum and mirror-descent-style metrics in one variational
framework. Betancourt, Jordan \& Wilson~\cite{betancourt2018symplectic}
and Maddison et al.~\cite{maddison2018hamiltonian} go further and cast
accelerated methods explicitly as (dissipative, symplectic, or conformal)
Hamiltonian systems on parameter-momentum phase space, with
Fran\c ca et al.~\cite{franca2021conformal} analyzing the conformal-symplectic
integrators that make such dynamics stable under discretization -- the same
concern this report's own energy-backtracking safeguard
(\S\ref{sec:quantum-benchmark}) addresses empirically rather than through a
structured integrator. Natural gradient~\cite{amari1998natural} and mirror
descent~\cite{nemirovski1983problem} are older, narrower instances of the
same idea: replace the Euclidean metric implicit in gradient descent with a
task-dependent Riemannian or Bregman geometry. Riemannian and manifold
optimization~\cite{absil2008optimization} develops optimization directly on
curved manifolds using retractions and parallel transport; this report's
$g_{ij}(\theta)$ and its curvature correction $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:fgeo}) are closely related to that literature's
Christoffel-symbol corrections for a moving metric, a connection worth
making precise in future work rather than only qualitatively here. A
distinction that must also be drawn explicitly: Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo~\cite{betancourt2017conceptual} uses the identical
coordinate/momentum/Hamiltonian machinery, but for \emph{sampling} from a
target distribution via conservative (energy-preserving) trajectories:
what distinguishes the present, optimization-oriented use is precisely the
dissipative modification (Rayleigh damping, Eq.~\eqref{eq:dissipative}) that
HMC deliberately avoids in order to preserve the target measure.

What is not already established in this literature, and is the actual
contribution tested here, is threefold. First, the prior work above is
almost entirely a \emph{descriptive} theory of existing accelerated methods
(showing that Nesterov, heavy-ball, and mirror descent already are
instances of a continuous-time dynamical system); this report instead
treats the dynamical system as a \emph{prescriptive} template for a new,
directly trainable optimizer with a metric built from the loss's own
regularized Hessian, an explicit non-Markovian memory force, and a
spectral-entropy regularizer on the metric's own eigenvalue spread --
none of which appear as a package in the works above. Second,
\S\ref{sec:modern-optimizers} extends the same correspondence to
optimizers published independently and after this line of theoretical
work (AdamW, Lion, Shampoo, Muon), which to our knowledge have not been
placed on a single metric/kinetic-term/potential-splitting axis elsewhere.
Third, \S\ref{sec:experimental-validation}--\S\ref{sec:robustness-followup}
subject the resulting optimizer to the same empirical stress-testing
(multi-seed variance, width scaling, ablation of each additive term) that a
purely theoretical ODE-limit analysis does not by itself provide, and report
the negative and mixed results (\S\ref{sec:robustness-followup},
Table~\ref{tab:quantum-ablation}) alongside the favorable ones.

\section{Mathematical Background}
\label{sec:mathematical-background}

\subsection{Coordinates, potential, and the Hamiltonian}

The framework treats model parameters $\theta = (\theta^1,\dots,\theta^n)$ as
generalized coordinates $q^i \equiv \theta^i$, with the loss function playing
the role of potential energy, $V(q) \equiv L(\theta)$. Given a metric
$g_{ij}(\theta)$ on parameter space defining a kinetic term
\begin{equation}
T = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\dot\theta^i\, g_{ij}(\theta)\, \dot\theta^j,
\end{equation}
the Legendre transform of the Lagrangian $\mathcal L = T - V$ gives canonical
momentum $p_i = \partial \mathcal L/\partial \dot\theta^i = g_{ij}\dot\theta^j$
and Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
H(\theta,p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\, p_i\, g^{ij}(\theta)\, p_j + L(\theta),
\label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
where $g^{ij}$ is the matrix inverse of $g_{ij}$, $g^{ij}g_{jk} = \delta^i_k$.

\subsection{Hamilton's equations and the geometric force}

Hamilton's equations $\dot\theta^i = \partial H/\partial p_i$,
$\dot p_i = -\partial H/\partial\theta^i$ applied to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian} give
\begin{align}
\dot\theta^i &= g^{ij}(\theta)\, p_j, \label{eq:theta-dot}\\
\dot p_i &= -\partial_i L - \tfrac{1}{2}\, p_j p_k\, \partial_i g^{jk}.
\label{eq:p-dot}
\end{align}
The second term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:p-dot} is named the \emph{geometric force},
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{geo}} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\, p^{\mathsf T}\!\left(\nabla_\theta g^{-1}\right) p,
\label{eq:fgeo}
\end{equation}
so that Eq.~\eqref{eq:p-dot} reads $\dot p_i = -\partial_i L - F_{\mathrm{geo}}$:
motion is driven by the loss gradient \emph{and} by how the metric itself
curves across parameter space.

\subsection{Dissipation}

Machine-learning optimization is not conservative -- the loss must decrease --
so a Rayleigh dissipation function $\mathcal R(p) = \tfrac12 \gamma\, p^i p_i$
is added, giving $\dot p_i = -\partial H/\partial\theta^i - \partial\mathcal
R/\partial p_i$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
\dot\theta^i = g^{ij}(\theta)\,p_j, \qquad
\dot p_i = -\partial_i L - F_{\mathrm{geo}} - \gamma p_i.
\label{eq:dissipative}
\end{equation}
In the flat Euclidean case ($g_{ij}=\delta_{ij}$, so $\partial_i g^{jk}=0$
identically and $F_{\mathrm{geo}}=0$), Eq.~\eqref{eq:dissipative} reduces to
$\dot\theta = p$, $\dot p = -\nabla_\theta L - \gamma p$, and in the overdamped
limit ($p \approx -\gamma^{-1}\nabla_\theta L$) this Euler-discretizes to plain
gradient descent. Classical momentum and Adam are presented as further special
cases: momentum as damped Hamiltonian flow with $p_t \sim v_t$; Adam as an
approximate \emph{diagonal}-metric Hamiltonian optimizer,
$g_t^{ij} \approx \delta^{ij}/(\sqrt{v_t^i}+\epsilon)$, with the explicit
caveat that Adam has no analogue of $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ since $v_t$ is treated
as locally constant.

\subsection{The generalized update rule}

With a memory (non-Markovian) correction built from an exponentially weighted
kernel over past gradients,
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{mem}}(\theta_t) = \mu \sum_{k=1}^{t} \kappa^{\,t-k}\, \nabla_\theta L(\theta_k),
\qquad 0 < \kappa < 1,
\label{eq:fmem}
\end{equation}
the proposed discretized optimizer is
\begin{align}
p_{t+1} &= \beta p_t - \eta\Big[\nabla_\theta L + F_{\mathrm{geo}} + F_{\mathrm{mem}}
  + \alpha \nabla_\theta S(g)\Big], \label{eq:update-p}\\
\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t + \eta\, g^{-1}\, p_{t+1}. \label{eq:update-theta}
\end{align}
The metric itself may be built from the (regularized) Hessian of the loss,
$g_{ij} = H_{ij} + \lambda\delta_{ij}$, $H_{ij} = \partial^2 L/\partial\theta^i
\partial\theta^j$, $\lambda > 0$.

\subsubsection*{Memory-metric term $M_{ij}$}
\label{sec:memory-metric}

The metric extension $g_{ij} = H_{ij} + \mu M_{ij}$ requires $M_{ij}$ to be a
rank-2 symmetric object, while Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} defines
$F_{\mathrm{mem}}$ as a vector force with one free index. A direct
vector-to-matrix lift is therefore needed before the memory contribution can
enter the metric.
Use the same exponentially weighted construction Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} already
uses, applied one power higher, to the
\emph{outer product} of the gradient with itself rather than to the gradient
alone:
\begin{equation}
M_{ij}(\theta_t) = \kappa\, M_{ij}(\theta_{t-1}) + \big(\partial_i L(\theta_t)\big)\big(\partial_j L(\theta_t)\big),
\label{eq:memory-metric}
\end{equation}
with the same decay $\kappa$ as Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} (so no new hyperparameter
is introduced) and $M_{ij}(\theta_0) = 0$. This is exactly the
uncentered empirical-Fisher construction used to motivate Adam's diagonal
metric below (\S\ref{sec:special-cases},
``RMSProp and Adam'') ($v_t \approx
\mathrm{diag}$ of an EMA of squared gradients) -- Eq.~\eqref{eq:memory-metric}
is the same recursion kept as a full matrix instead of truncated to its
diagonal. Three properties make it a legitimate metric contribution rather
than an arbitrary patch: (1) it is manifestly symmetric,
$M_{ij}=M_{ji}$, since $(\partial_i L)(\partial_j L) = (\partial_j
L)(\partial_i L)$; (2) it is positive semidefinite for any gradient sequence,
since it is a nonnegative sum of rank-1 PSD terms $vv^{\mathsf T}$, so
$g_{ij}=H_{ij}+\lambda\delta_{ij}+\mu M_{ij}$ can only add curvature, never
break the positive-definiteness already established for $H$; and (3) it
reduces to exactly $F_{\mathrm{mem}}$'s own recursion in the one-dimensional
case, $M(\theta_t) = \kappa M(\theta_{t-1}) + (\partial L)^2$, matching
Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} squared term-by-term. Thus the memory metric is not an
additional free structure: it is the natural rank-2 lift of the same
exponential-memory law already present in the vector force.

\section{Standard Optimizers as Special Cases}
\label{sec:special-cases}

The unifying claim is structural: standard optimizer families appear by
choosing particular metrics, damping limits, and force terms in the
Hamiltonian--geometric update. The master form is
\begin{align}
p_{t+1} &= \beta p_t - \eta\left[\nabla_\theta L
  + F_{\mathrm{geo}} + F_{\mathrm{mem}} + \alpha\nabla_\theta S(g)\right],\\
\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t + \eta g^{-1}(\theta_t)p_{t+1}.
\end{align}
Different optimizers are obtained by deleting or restricting pieces of this
system:

\subsection*{Gradient descent}

Set the metric to the Euclidean metric, $g=I$, remove memory and spectral
forces, and note that $\nabla_\theta g^{-1}=0$, so $F_{\mathrm{geo}}=0$. The
dissipative continuous-time Hamiltonian system reduces to
\begin{equation}
\dot\theta = p,\qquad \dot p = -\nabla_\theta L-\gamma p.
\end{equation}
In the overdamped limit, momentum equilibrates much faster than position:
$\dot p\approx0$, hence
\begin{equation}
p\approx-\gamma^{-1}\nabla_\theta L.
\end{equation}
Substitution into $\dot\theta=p$ gives
\begin{equation}
\dot\theta\approx-\gamma^{-1}\nabla_\theta L.
\end{equation}
Forward Euler with step size $h$ therefore gives
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\frac{h}{\gamma}\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t)
           =\theta_t-\eta\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),
\end{equation}
which is ordinary gradient descent.

\subsection*{Heavy-ball momentum}

Keep the same flat metric $g=I$ and $F_{\mathrm{geo}}=F_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\alpha\nabla S(g)=0$, but do not take the overdamped limit. The master update
becomes
\begin{align}
p_{t+1} &= \beta p_t-\eta\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),\\
\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t+\eta p_{t+1}.
\end{align}
Define the usual descent velocity $v_t=-p_t$. Then
\begin{align}
v_{t+1} &= \beta v_t+\eta\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),\\
\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t-\eta v_{t+1}.
\end{align}
Up to the conventional placement of the learning rate inside or outside the
velocity variable, this is the heavy-ball momentum update.

\subsection*{Nesterov-style momentum}

Nesterov momentum uses the same flat damped Hamiltonian skeleton but evaluates
the gradient at a lookahead point. Starting from the heavy-ball reduction,
replace the force evaluation point by
\begin{equation}
\tilde\theta_t=\theta_t-\beta v_t.
\end{equation}
The update becomes
\begin{align}
v_{t+1} &= \beta v_t+\eta\nabla_\theta L(\tilde\theta_t),\\
\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t-\eta v_{t+1}.
\end{align}
Thus Nesterov is not a new metric; it is a lookahead discretization of the
same flat dissipative Hamiltonian momentum flow.

\subsection*{RMSProp and Adam}

RMSProp~\cite{tieleman2012rmsprop} and Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam}: let the
metric be diagonal and time-adaptive:
\begin{equation}
g_t=\operatorname{diag}\left(\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon\right),
\qquad
g_t^{-1}=\operatorname{diag}\left((\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon)^{-1}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\hat v_t$ is an exponential moving average of squared gradients. With
momentum $m_t$ identified with the descent momentum, the metric-preconditioned
step is
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1}
  =\theta_t-\eta\,g_t^{-1}m_t
  =\theta_t-\eta\,\frac{m_t}{\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon}.
\end{equation}
This is the Adam/RMSProp form. The correspondence is deliberately qualified:
Adam treats $g_t$ as locally fixed during the step, so it drops
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{geo}}
  =\tfrac12 p^{\mathsf T}(\nabla_\theta g^{-1})p.
\end{equation}
Therefore Adam is a diagonal-metric approximation to the Hamiltonian optimizer,
not the full Hamiltonian-geometric optimizer.

The same Adam form can also be derived variationally from a local
metric-weighted step principle. At iteration $t$, approximate the loss by its
first-order expansion around $\theta_t$ and penalize motion using a positive
diagonal metric $G_t$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta_t
  = \arg\min_{\Delta}
  \left[
    m_t^{\mathsf T}\Delta
    + \frac{1}{2\eta}\Delta^{\mathsf T}G_t\Delta
  \right].
\label{eq:adam-variational-objective}
\end{equation}
Here $m_t$ is the exponentially averaged gradient direction and $G_t$ is the
local geometry used to measure the cost of a parameter displacement. The
stationarity condition is
\begin{equation}
0=\nabla_\Delta\left[
    m_t^{\mathsf T}\Delta
    + \frac{1}{2\eta}\Delta^{\mathsf T}G_t\Delta
  \right]
  =m_t+\eta^{-1}G_t\Delta,
\end{equation}
so
\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta_t=-\eta G_t^{-1}m_t,
\qquad
\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t+\Delta\theta_t.
\end{equation}
Choosing
\begin{equation}
G_t=\operatorname{diag}\left(\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon\right)
\end{equation}
gives
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1}
  = \theta_t-\eta\,\frac{m_t}{\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon},
\end{equation}
which is Adam's parameter update after bias-correcting $m_t$ and $\hat v_t$.
This derivation makes the variational meaning explicit: Adam chooses the step
that most decreases the local linearized loss per unit diagonal-metric
movement cost. In Hamiltonian language, it is the diagonal, locally frozen,
metric-preconditioned variational step. What it still does not include is the
curvature force that appears when the metric is differentiated as a function of
the parameters.

\subsection*{Natural gradient}

Natural gradient descent~\cite{amari1998natural}: choose the metric to be an
information metric, usually the Fisher information
matrix $F(\theta)$, and remove Hamiltonian momentum, memory, spectral force,
and geometric-force dynamics. The pure metric-preconditioned descent step is
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1}
  =\theta_t-\eta\,F(\theta_t)^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),
\end{equation}
which is natural gradient descent. It is the full-metric, no-momentum limit of
the same geometry.

\subsection*{Entropy descent}

Use a positive-definite metric $g(\theta)$ and keep the inverse-metric
preconditioner and spectral diagnostic, but set $\beta=0$ and remove memory and
the explicit geometric force. Then
\begin{equation}
p_{t+1}=-\eta\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),
\qquad
\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta^2 g^{-1}(\theta_t)\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t).
\end{equation}
Absorbing one factor of $\eta$ into the effective step size gives
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1}
  =\theta_t-\eta_{\mathrm{eff}}g^{-1}(\theta_t)\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),
\end{equation}
the entropy-descent / metric-preconditioned update used in the experiments.

The Hamiltonian-geometric optimizer is the general member of this family:
it contains the metric, momentum, curvature, memory, and spectral forces in
one update law.

\section{Modern Optimizers as Further Derivations}
\label{sec:modern-optimizers}

\S\ref{sec:special-cases} derives classical optimizers: gradient descent,
momentum, Adam/RMSProp, natural gradient, and entropy descent. To test whether
the framework also captures algorithms outside this classical group, the same
geometric language is extended below to independently developed modern
optimizers. AdamW, Lion, Shampoo/K-FAC, and Muon arise from specific choices
of metric, kinetic term, or potential splitting; AdaFactor and LARS/LAMB then
complete the hierarchy of retained metric structure.

\subsection*{AdamW: decoupled weight decay as a split metric}

AdamW (Loshchilov \& Hutter, \emph{Decoupled Weight Decay Regularization},
ICLR 2019)~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} updates
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta\left[\frac{\hat m_t}{\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon} + \lambda\theta_t\right],
\label{eq:adamw}
\end{equation}
and is empirically distinct from plain Adam applied to the $L_2$-regularized
loss $L(\theta)+\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\theta\|^2$, whose gradient
$\nabla_\theta L + \lambda\theta$ gets divided by $\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon$
\emph{in its entirety} -- the regularizer term is contaminated by the adaptive
scaling meant for the loss term. Eq.~\eqref{eq:adamw} keeps the two terms
separately scaled. This is exactly the potential splitting a Hamiltonian
system uses for a separable potential $V=V_1+V_2$: evolve each piece with its
\emph{own} metric via Lie--Trotter operator splitting rather than sharing one
metric across the whole potential. Take $V_1=L(\theta)$ with the diagonal
Adam metric $g_1^{-1}=\operatorname{diag}((\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon)^{-1})$
from \S\ref{sec:special-cases}, and $V_2=\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\theta\|^2$ with
the \emph{flat} metric $g_2=I$ (the same flat-metric case that reduces the
framework to plain gradient descent, \S\ref{sec:special-cases}). One
Lie--Trotter half-step under each potential,
\begin{equation}
\theta' = \theta_t - \eta\, g_1^{-1}\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t),
\qquad
\theta_{t+1} = \theta' - \eta\, g_2^{-1}(\lambda\theta'),
\label{eq:adamw-split}
\end{equation}
reproduces Eq.~\eqref{eq:adamw} to first order in $\eta$ ($\theta'\approx\theta_t$
inside the second half-step). AdamW is therefore the framework with a
\emph{piecewise} metric -- adaptive on the data-fit term, flat on the
regularizer -- rather than a single shared metric, which is precisely the
mechanism used in the adaptive-optimization literature to explain why decoupled
weight decay differs from $L_2$ regularization in adaptive methods, now
expressed as a metric choice.

\subsection*{Lion: an $L_1$ kinetic term}

Lion (Chen et al., \emph{Symbolic Discovery of Optimization Algorithms},
NeurIPS 2023)~\cite{chen2023lion} updates
\begin{align}
c_t &= \beta_1 m_{t-1} + (1-\beta_1)\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t), \notag\\
\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t - \eta\,\operatorname{sign}(c_t),
\label{eq:lion-step}\\
m_t &= \beta_2 m_{t-1} + (1-\beta_2)\nabla_\theta L(\theta_t). \notag
\end{align}
Every optimizer in \S\ref{sec:special-cases} keeps the Hamiltonian's kinetic
term quadratic, $T=\tfrac12\dot\theta^i g_{ij}\dot\theta^j$, so
$\dot\theta=\partial H/\partial p=g^{-1}p$ is always linear in $p$. Lion's
$\operatorname{sign}(\cdot)$ dynamics require dropping that assumption:
take the flat metric $g=I$ (so $F_{\mathrm{geo}}=0$, as in gradient descent)
but replace the quadratic kinetic term with the $L_1$ kinetic term
$T=\sum_i|p_i|$. The Legendre transform still applies to a non-quadratic
convex kinetic term; Hamilton's equation for $\theta$ becomes
\begin{equation}
\dot\theta^i = \frac{\partial T}{\partial p_i} = \operatorname{sign}(p_i),
\label{eq:l1-kinetic}
\end{equation}
the subgradient of $|p_i|$ at $p_i\neq0$, while $\dot p=-\nabla_\theta L$
is unchanged (Eq.~\eqref{eq:dissipative}'s flat-metric case, without the
dissipation term since Lion has no separate $\beta$ damping coefficient
distinct from its momentum decay). Discretizing $\dot p=-\nabla_\theta L$
with an exponential moving average recovers $m_t$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:lion-step}'s third line, matching Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem}'s own
EMA structure with $\kappa=\beta_2$); Eq.~\eqref{eq:l1-kinetic} discretized
at a \emph{second}, independently-decayed momentum estimate $c_t$ gives
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lion-step}'s sign step. That second, independently-decayed
estimate is a genuine extension beyond what \S\ref{sec:mathematical-background}
proposes (which uses one decay constant $\kappa$ for its one memory term) --
Lion needs \emph{two} decay constants, $\beta_1$ for the vector fed to the
kinetic term's gradient and $\beta_2$ for the persisted momentum -- not a
contradiction of the framework, but a two-timescale generalization of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} it does not by itself contain.

\subsection*{Shampoo and K-FAC: Kronecker-factored metrics}

For a matrix-shaped parameter $W\in\mathbb R^{m\times n}$ (one network layer),
Shampoo (Gupta, Koren \& Singer, \emph{Shampoo: Preconditioned Stochastic
Tensor Optimization}, ICML 2018)~\cite{gupta2018shampoo} maintains
$L_t=\sum_\tau G_\tau G_\tau^{\mathsf T}\in\mathbb R^{m\times m}$,
$R_t=\sum_\tau G_\tau^{\mathsf T} G_\tau\in\mathbb R^{n\times n}$ from the
gradient matrices $G_\tau$, and updates $W_{t+1}=W_t-\eta\,
L_t^{-1/4}G_tR_t^{-1/4}$. Using
$\operatorname{vec}(AXB)=(B^{\mathsf T}\otimes A)\operatorname{vec}(X)$, this is
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{vec}(W_{t+1}) = \operatorname{vec}(W_t) - \eta\,
\big(R_t^{-1/4}\otimes L_t^{-1/4}\big)\operatorname{vec}(G_t),
\label{eq:shampoo}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ exactly $\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta\,g^{-1}\nabla_\theta L$
(the flat-metric, momentum-free case of Eq.~\eqref{eq:update-theta}, natural
gradient's own form in \S\ref{sec:special-cases}) with the full
$(mn)\times(mn)$ metric $g^{-1}$ restricted to the Kronecker-factored family
$g^{-1}=R^{-1/2}\otimes L^{-1/2}$: the same regularized-Hessian metric idea
as $g_{ij}=H_{ij}+\lambda\delta_{ij}$ (\S\ref{sec:mathematical-background}),
just a \emph{structured} approximation that cuts inversion cost from
$O((mn)^3)$ to $O(m^3+n^3)$ by exploiting the parameter's matrix shape. K-FAC
(Martens \& Grosse, \emph{Optimizing Neural Networks with Kronecker-factored
Approximate Curvature}, ICML 2015)~\cite{martens2015kfac} is the same
block-Kronecker metric family with its two factors built from the actual
Fisher information instead ($A=\mathbb E[aa^{\mathsf T}]$ over layer inputs,
$S=\mathbb E[ss^{\mathsf T}]$ over pre-activation gradients, Fisher
$\approx A\otimes S$) -- a costlier but more principled member of the same
structured-metric family, natural gradient with a tractable Kronecker
approximation to $F(\theta)$ rather than the full matrix in
\S\ref{sec:special-cases}.

\subsection*{Muon: whitened momentum as a spectral-entropy limit}

Muon (Jordan et al., 2024, \emph{Muon: An optimizer for hidden layers in
neural networks}~\cite{jordan2024muon}; scaled up in Jordan et al., \emph{Muon
is Scalable for LLM Training}~\cite{jordan2025muonscalable}) updates $M_t=\mu M_{t-1}+G_t$, then replaces
$M_t$ by its orthogonal polar factor $O_t=M_t(M_t^{\mathsf T}M_t)^{-1/2}$
(computed by Newton--Schulz iteration in the original algorithm, avoiding an
explicit eigendecomposition for GPU efficiency at scale; the implementation
used for Table~\ref{tab:modern-optimizers} instead computes $O_t$ via exact
SVD, since the matrices here are small enough that this is a mathematically
identical but simpler substitute, not a faithful runtime reproduction of
Muon's actual deployed cost profile). Write $M_t=U\Sigma
V^{\mathsf T}$, so $O_t=UV^{\mathsf T}$: orthogonalizing replaces every
nonzero singular value of the momentum by exactly $1$. Consider the
$n\times n$ right-side metric built from the momentum itself,
$g_M^{-1}=(M_t^{\mathsf T}M_t)^{-1/2}$, so that $O_t=M_tg_M^{-1}$
(equivalently, the $m\times m$ left-side metric $(M_tM_t^{\mathsf
T})^{-1/2}$ gives the same $O_t$ via $O_t=(M_tM_t^{\mathsf T})^{-1/2}M_t$ --
the polar factor is the unique matrix reachable by whitening either side,
unlike Shampoo's genuinely two-sided $L^{-1/4}GR^{-1/4}$). $g_M^{-1}$ has
eigenvalues $\tilde\lambda_i=\sigma_i^{-1}$ where $\sigma_i$ are $M_t$'s
singular values; the spectral entropy Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral-entropy} of
this induced metric is maximized exactly when all $\tilde\lambda_i$ are
equal, i.e.\ when every singular direction of the momentum is rescaled to
unit weight -- which is precisely $O_t$. Muon is therefore the framework's own
$\alpha\nabla_\theta S(g)$ regularizer (\S\ref{sec:spectral-entropy}) taken to
its $\alpha\to\infty$ limit -- not softly nudging the metric toward equal
eigenvalues, but hard-constraining it there every step -- applied to a metric
built from the running momentum's own second-moment structure rather than
from the Hessian. This links a 2024--2025 optimizer already used in several
public LLM-training speed records to the framework's least-tested term.

\subsection*{AdaFactor and LAMB/LARS: coarser structured metrics}

AdaFactor (Shazeer \& Stern, \emph{Adafactor: Adaptive Learning Rates with
Sublinear Memory Cost}, ICML 2018)~\cite{shazeer2018adafactor} stores only row and
column accumulators $r\in\mathbb R^m$, $c\in\mathbb R^n$ for a matrix
parameter and reconstructs a rank-1 approximation $V\approx rc^{\mathsf
T}/\mathbf 1^{\mathsf T}r$ to Adam's diagonal second moment: the
\S\ref{sec:special-cases} diagonal metric family, further restricted to a
rank-1-factored diagonal to cut its storage from $O(mn)$ to $O(m+n)$. LARS
(You, Gitman \& Ginsburg, 2017)~\cite{you2017lars} and LAMB (You et al.,
\emph{Large Batch Optimization for Deep Learning}, ICLR 2020)~\cite{you2020lamb} rescale each layer's update by a per-layer trust ratio
$\|\theta_{\mathrm{layer}}\|/\|\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{layer}}\|$: a metric
restricted to a single scalar per parameter block, coarser than Adam's
per-coordinate diagonal, chosen adaptively each step rather than accumulated.
Table~\ref{tab:metric-hierarchy} places the optimizer families on one
axis: how much of the true $(mn)\times(mn)$ metric each family keeps.

\subsection{Spectral entropy}
\label{sec:spectral-entropy}

Decomposing $g = U\Sigma U^{\mathsf T}$ with eigenvalues
$\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n$, the \emph{spectral entropy}
\begin{equation}
S(g) = -\sum_i \tilde\lambda_i \log \tilde\lambda_i, \qquad
\tilde\lambda_i = \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_j \lambda_j},
\label{eq:spectral-entropy}
\end{equation}
measures the spread of curvature directions in parameter space. Adding a
regularizer $R_{\mathrm{spec}} = \alpha S(g)$ to the Hamiltonian,
\begin{equation}
H(\theta,p) = \tfrac12 p^{\mathsf T} g^{-1}(\theta) p + L(\theta) + \alpha S(g),
\label{eq:hamiltonian-spectral}
\end{equation}
one obtains
$\dot p = -\nabla_\theta L - \alpha \nabla_\theta S(g) - F_{\mathrm{geo}}$.
This follows by differentiating Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian-spectral} directly:
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial H}{\partial\theta^i}
  &= \tfrac12\, p^{\mathsf T}\Big(\partial_i g^{-1}\Big) p
     + \partial_i L + \alpha\, \partial_i S(g) \notag\\
  &= F_{\mathrm{geo},i} + \partial_i L + \alpha\,\partial_i S(g),
\label{eq:spectral-force-derivation}\\
\dot p_i &= -\frac{\partial H}{\partial \theta^i}
  = -\partial_i L - \alpha\, \partial_i S(g) - F_{\mathrm{geo},i}.
  \label{eq:spectral-force-corrected}
\end{align}
The geometric-force contribution therefore enters the momentum equation with
negative sign. This is the convention used throughout the discrete update:
the loss gradient, geometric force, memory force, and spectral force are
assembled into one bracket subtracted with a single overall minus sign,
matching Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral-force-corrected}.

\section{Analytical Validation}
\label{sec:review}

Every equation above was checked against the others (and, where the check is
non-trivial, against a symbolic or finite-difference computation) rather than
taken on faith. Two points require explicit choices for a complete
formulation: the sign of the geometric force in the spectral Hamiltonian,
and the construction of a rank-2 memory metric from a rank-1 memory force.
Two additional points are harmless modelling ambiguities. The corresponding
numerical checks are reproduced in Table~\ref{tab:numeric-checks}.

\subsection*{\finding{Sign convention for $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ in the spectral Hamiltonian}}

As derived in \S\ref{sec:spectral-entropy}, differentiating
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian-spectral} directly gives
Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral-force-corrected}, $\dot p_i = -\partial_i L -
\alpha\partial_i S(g) - F_{\mathrm{geo},i}$. This is not a new convention:
the discrete update uses the same sign by assembling the loss gradient,
geometric force, memory force, and spectral force into one bracket subtracted
with a single overall minus sign.

\subsection*{\finding{Construction of the memory metric $M_{ij}$}}

Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} defines $F_{\mathrm{mem}}(\theta_t)$ as a sum of gradients
$\nabla_\theta L(\theta_k)$, a rank-1 object with a single free index. By
contrast, the metric term $M_{ij}$ must be rank-2 and symmetric in order to
be added to $H_{ij}$. A vector-to-matrix construction is therefore required.
\S\ref{sec:memory-metric} gives an explicit construction,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:memory-metric}: an EMA of gradient outer products with the same
decay $\kappa$ Eq.~\eqref{eq:fmem} already uses, which is manifestly symmetric
and PSD and reduces to $F_{\mathrm{mem}}$'s own recursion in one dimension.
The construction is symmetric, positive semidefinite, and agrees with the
closed-form recursion above. It is not used in the benchmark tables in
\S\ref{sec:experimental-validation}, so none of those results depend on it.

\subsection*{\finding{Coordinate form of Rayleigh dissipation}}

$\mathcal R(p) = \tfrac12\gamma\, p^i p_i$ is written without specifying
whether the implicit contraction uses $g^{ij}$, $\delta^{ij}$, or a separate
friction tensor. For a genuinely curved $g_{ij}(\theta)$, $\sum_i p_i^2$ is not
coordinate-invariant, so the damping law is ambiguous off the flat case even
though it reproduces the intended $-\gamma p_i$ term in every case
worked out in the paper (all of which use $g=\delta$). A coordinate-invariant
choice would use $\mathcal R(\theta,\dot\theta) = \tfrac12\gamma\,
g_{ij}(\theta)\dot\theta^i\dot\theta^j$, which reduces to the same $\gamma p_i$
term via $p = g\dot\theta$, and closes the ambiguity (verified numerically in
Table~\ref{tab:numeric-checks}, Rayleigh curved-metric row).

\subsection*{\finding{Gradient of spectral entropy}}

Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral-entropy} defines $S(g)$ for a fixed $g$; differentiating
it with respect to $\theta$ requires differentiating the eigenvalues of a
$\theta$-dependent matrix (standard, but non-trivial, eigenvalue-perturbation
calculus), which the paper never carries out. The term is well-posed once
completed -- confirmed by matching a closed-form eigenvalue-perturbation
gradient against a finite-difference gradient
(Table~\ref{tab:numeric-checks}, spectral-entropy-gradient row) -- but
must be included explicitly when the spectral force is used.

\section{Computational Form}

The computational update follows
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:update-p}--\eqref{eq:update-theta} directly, with:
\begin{itemize}
\item Positive-definite metric construction: symmetrizes any candidate metric,
  $g \to \tfrac12(g+g^{\mathsf T})$, then adds regularization
  $\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} = \lambda + \max(0,-\lambda_{\min})$ to guarantee
  positive-definiteness for an indefinite Hessian, addressing the
  positive-definiteness finding above Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian}.
\item Geometric-force evaluation: computes
  $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:fgeo}) by central differences of $g^{-1}$,
  so it works for any $\theta$-dependent metric, not only a fixed-feature one.
\item Spectral entropy: Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral-entropy} directly from
  the eigenvalues of the (regularized, symmetrized) metric.
\item Hamiltonian-geometric update: assembles the full force, subtracts it in
  the momentum equation, and advances parameters through the inverse metric --
  the sign convention derived in \S\ref{sec:review}.
\end{itemize}

methodological details that should be stated explicitly.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Reproducibility.} Optimizer-specific minibatch seeds are fixed

\item \textbf{Learning-rate calibration.} The Hamiltonian-geometric update

momentum to a parameter displacement (Eqs.~33--34; see

epoch should be close to the best epoch, indicating convergence rather than
late oscillation.

\subsection{Modern Optimizer Benchmark}
\label{sec:modern-benchmark}

\S\ref{sec:modern-optimizers} derives Lion, AdamW, Shampoo, and Muon as
special cases of the same update family symbolically; this section runs them
after checking their defining properties -- the $L_1$-kinetic sign bound, the
decoupled-decay/L2 distinction, the $\operatorname{vec}(AXB)$ Kronecker
identity, and the spectral-entropy maximality of orthogonalization -- on the same spiral MLP as
Table~\ref{tab:deepobs-local}, so the comparison sits on real matrix-shaped
weights (needed for Shampoo's and Muon's structure) rather than the flattened
linear PINN model. Every optimizer's learning rate was swept on this exact
benchmark with equal effort -- none, including the baselines, are left at
arbitrary library defaults -- and every optimizer is scored by its
\emph{best-epoch} validation loss rather than the final epoch, for the same
reason as \S\ref{sec:quantum-benchmark}: Figure~\ref{fig:modern-optimizers}
shows every optimizer's validation loss oscillating in a noise band for most
of training, so whichever one happens to land lowest on the last epoch is not
a meaningful winner by itself.

\textbf{The precise meaning of ``Hamiltonian-geometric'' in this comparison
is narrower than the full framework.} The implementation used here keeps only the
diagonal adaptive metric and momentum: $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ is not computed at
all, the memory force is disabled, and there is no spectral term. By this report's own
\S\ref{sec:special-cases} correspondence, that reduction is already
described as ``a diagonal-metric approximation to the Hamiltonian
optimizer,'' i.e.\ close in spirit to Adam with a different momentum
discretization. The near-tie with Adam in
Table~\ref{tab:modern-optimizers} is therefore not surprising in the way a
reader might assume from the framework's full generality -- it is closer to
two similar diagonal-preconditioned methods agreeing with each other than to
the paper's distinctive curvature/memory/entropy machinery outperforming
Adam. The full machinery ($F_{\mathrm{geo}}$, $F_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the
spectral term) is exercised only on the PINN benchmark (exact Hessian
metric, Table~\ref{tab:pinn}) and the quantum-control benchmark
(\S\ref{sec:quantum-benchmark}), not here.

For the kicked-top task, each reported value is the best loss attained along
the training trajectory rather than the last iterate. This convention is
applied identically to all optimizers and is appropriate for a chaotic
control landscape, where nearby control sequences can lead to substantially
different terminal states and the final iterate may not represent the best
state visited during training.

The Hamiltonian-geometric run also uses an energy-backtracking safeguard. In
a dissipative Hamiltonian system, energy should not increase under the
continuous-time dynamics; finite-step discretization can violate this
property on rugged landscapes. The safeguard damps and recomputes steps that
increase the loss, providing a discrete analogue of additional Rayleigh
dissipation. With this mechanism, larger momentum and learning-rate values
can be used without accepting loss-increasing overshoot. A five-seed
robustness check found that this tuned configuration outperformed
heavy-ball/AdamW on three seeds and underperformed on two. The result should
therefore be read as evidence that energy control is useful on this task, not
as a claim of unconditional dominance in chaotic quantum control.

\textbf{This safeguard is not free, and the cost is not hidden:} each
backtrack retries the position update with damped momentum, so an accepted
Hamiltonian-geometric step can cost up to
31 loss evaluations against exactly
$1$ for every baseline in Table~\ref{tab:quantum}. That is precisely why the
table reports wall-clock runtime rather than iteration count: Hamiltonian-geometric's
$9.996\,\mathrm{s}$ against $3.0$--$6.0\,\mathrm{s}$ for the baselines already
reflects this extra work, and the correct reading of the comparison is
runtime-for-runtime, not step-for-step.

\subsection{Metric-structure scaling: does richness of $g$ predict the advantage?}
\label{sec:metric-structure-scaling}

The condition-scaling result above (Table~\ref{tab:condition-scaling}) compares
different \emph{optimizers}, each with its own fixed metric structure, so it
cannot isolate whether the advantage comes from having a full metric per se or
from some other difference between the algorithms. This experiment holds the
optimizer fixed (Hamiltonian-geometric, diagonal-momentum term off) and varies
only how much of the same underlying Hessian $a$ the metric $g$ is allowed to
see, on the identical rotated quadratic used above: \textbf{scalar}
($g=\bar\kappa I$, $\bar\kappa=\operatorname{tr}(a)/n$), \textbf{diagonal}
($g=\operatorname{diag}(a)$), \textbf{block} ($g$ restricted to $3\times3$
diagonal blocks of $a$, zero elsewhere), and \textbf{full} ($g=a$). Task
dimension $n=12$, 10 seeds per cell (fresh random $\theta_0$ and rotation per
seed), 160 steps, identical learning rate across all four structures so the
comparison is not tuned per structure.

\section{Architecture Ablation: A Controlled Statistical Study}
\label{sec:architecture-ablation}

The quantum-control ablation above isolates several architectural choices on
one chaotic workload, but it is a single-seed estimate and therefore does not
provide variance estimates or formal hypothesis tests. The capacitor PINN and
DeepOBS/AlgoPerf-style benchmarks are also not ideal for ablation of
$F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ or $\nabla_\theta S(g)$, because their metrics are either
fixed-feature (constant in $\theta$, making both terms zero) or diagonal
adaptive accumulators. This section therefore studies the three additive
architectural terms -- $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$, $F_{\mathrm{mem}}$, and
$\alpha\nabla_\theta S(g)$ -- on a seeded synthetic quartic loss with a
genuinely $\theta$-dependent metric, repeated random seeds, and paired
hypothesis tests.

\subsection{Design}

Two metric choices are compared, because the metric choice itself turns out to
matter as much as which correction terms are switched on:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{toy metric}: an independently constructed,
  always-positive-definite, $\theta$-dependent matrix used only to
  validate the $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ and $\nabla_\theta S(g)$ formulas against
  finite differences. It is \emph{not} the Hessian of the loss, and is not expected to be a
  good preconditioner for it.
\item \textbf{Hessian metric}: the actual regularized Hessian of
  the loss, $g=Q+\operatorname{diag}(1.2\,\theta^2)$, regularized by the same
  positive-definite metric construction used throughout the computational rule --
  i.e.\ the direct Hessian-metric prescription.
\end{itemize}
For each metric, a full $2\times2\times2$ factorial crosses
$G\in\{\text{off},\text{on}\}$ (geometric correction),
$M\in\{\text{off},\text{on}\}$ (memory correction), and
$S\in\{\text{off},\text{on}\}$ (spectral weight $0$ vs.\ $0.05$), giving 8
configurations per metric. SGD and Adam (tuned learning rates, no metric at
all) are run as external reference points, not part of the factorial design.
Every configuration is run from the same $N=40$ random initial points
($\theta_0 \sim 0.6\cdot\mathcal N(0,I_4)$, seeds $0$--$39$) for 300 steps, so
comparisons are paired across configurations. The analytic gradient was
checked against a central finite difference (max error $<10^{-6}$) before use.

\subsection{Interpretation}

Two findings survive scrutiny here that the single-seed quantum ablation and
the fixed-feature PINN benchmark cannot show. First, whether a correction term
is beneficial depends on whether the metric is related to the loss's own
curvature: the same three terms are nearly inert under an unrelated
(but valid, positive-definite, $\theta$-dependent) metric, and one of them
(the spectral term) becomes highly significant once the metric is the loss's
real Hessian. Second, and more consequentially, the terms interact as a
stability unit rather than as independently additive improvements: the
factorial ANOVA finds no significant interactions on average log-loss, yet
the divergence-count table shows that using \emph{some but not all} of the
three terms together is exactly when rare catastrophic divergence appears
under the Hessian metric, while using all three (or none) is safe. An
average-effects ANOVA on log-loss is blind to this because catastrophic
outliers are compressed by the log transform and diluted by the 35+ seeds
that behave normally in every configuration; the raw divergence count is the
statistic that exposes it. Neither the toy-metric run (which never
diverges) nor the single-seed quantum ablation (which cannot see a rate at
all) could have revealed this.

\section{Symmetry Structure of the Metric}

The framework's correctness depends on $g_{ij}(\theta)$ being a valid
metric (symmetric, positive-definite) at every step. Two symmetry facts,
verified directly rather than asserted, justify why symmetrizing the metric
loses no information the dynamics could have used:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The antisymmetric part of any candidate metric is dynamically
inert.} Both the kinetic term $\tfrac12 p^{\mathsf T} g^{-1} p$ and the
geometric force $F_{\mathrm{geo}} = \tfrac12 p^{\mathsf T}(\nabla_\theta g^{-1})p$
are bilinear forms contracted with $p\otimes p$. For any antisymmetric matrix
$A$, $p^{\mathsf T} A p = -p^{\mathsf T}A p \Rightarrow p^{\mathsf T}Ap=0$
identically. Measured directly: across 14 random momentum vectors, $|p^{\mathsf
T}Ap| \le 8.9\times10^{-16}$ (floating-point zero) in every trial, while
$p^{\mathsf T}Sp$ (the symmetric part) varies substantially trial to trial
(Figure~\ref{fig:symmetry}, bottom-left).
\item \textbf{Spectral entropy is rotationally invariant.} $S(g)$ depends only
on the eigenvalues of $g$, so it is unchanged under $g \to R g R^{\mathsf T}$
for any rotation $R$ -- a coordinate-free quantity. Measured directly: over a
full $360^\circ$ rotation of a regularized metric $g$, $S(g)$ varies by
$2.2\times10^{-15}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:symmetry}, bottom-right).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Scale.} Every benchmark in this report is small: MLPs with a
few dozen hidden units, a 64-dimensional fixed-feature PINN, a 14-angle
quantum control sequence. The full $n\times n$ metric this framework depends
on for its clearest advantages (Table~\ref{tab:condition-scaling}) is
tractable at these sizes but is not a scalable substitute for Adam-family
optimizers on models with millions or billions of parameters; every result
here about a full-matrix metric should be read as evidence about the
\emph{mechanism}, not as a claim that it scales to production-size networks.
The scaling sweep in \S\ref{sec:robustness-followup} is the one direct test
of this concern, and it is unfavorable: Hamiltonian-geometric's advantage at
the tuned width does not persist as width grows.
\item \textbf{Seed coverage is uneven across benchmarks.} The multi-seed
check (10 seeds, \S\ref{sec:robustness-followup}) and the quantum robustness
check (5 seeds, \S\ref{sec:quantum-benchmark}) are the only places variance
across random seeds is reported; the four classical tables
(Tables~\ref{tab:mnist}--\ref{tab:pinn}) and the quantum ablation
(Table~\ref{tab:quantum-ablation}) are single-seed point estimates. $n=10$
seeds is itself underpowered to resolve the near-tie with Adam and Muon
decisively (\S\ref{sec:robustness-followup}).
\item \textbf{Hyperparameter tuning is a small grid search, not a proof of
optimality.} Every optimizer's learning rate in every benchmark was found by
sweeping a modest number of values on that same benchmark; the
learning-rate sensitivity curves (\S\ref{sec:robustness-followup}) show this
grid was in at least one case (the modern-optimizer benchmark) coarser than
ideal. Tuning effort was kept comparable across optimizers within each
benchmark, but is not identical in the sense of an exhaustive or
seed-averaged search for any of them.
\item \textbf{The energy-backtracking safeguard changes the compute budget,
not just the trajectory.} An accepted Hamiltonian-geometric step on the
quantum benchmark can cost up to $31$ loss evaluations against exactly $1$
for every baseline (\S\ref{sec:quantum-benchmark}); the reported wall-clock
runtimes account for this, but iteration counts alone would not.
\item \textbf{The memory-metric term $M_{ij}$ was tested on exactly one real
benchmark} (the quantum kicked-top task, \S\ref{sec:robustness-followup})
and found to hurt there. This is one negative data point, not a general
characterization of when the term would or would not help on other losses.
\item \textbf{The modern-optimizer benchmark's Hamiltonian-geometric
configuration is a diagonal-metric reduction}, not the full architecture
with $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$, $F_{\mathrm{mem}}$, and the spectral term active
(\S\ref{sec:modern-benchmark}); the full architecture is exercised only on
the exact-Hessian PINN benchmark and the quantum-control benchmark.
Consequently, the near-tie with Adam and Muon in
\S\ref{sec:robustness-followup} is not evidence that the framework's
distinctive curvature/memory/entropy machinery outperforms modern adaptive
methods -- only that a diagonal reduction of it does about as well as they
do.
\item \textbf{No large-scale, standard deep-learning benchmark (ImageNet-scale
vision, language-model pretraining) is included.} The AlgoPerf- and
DeepOBS-style benchmarks here are local, small-scale re-implementations
inspired by those suites, not the official harnesses, because the official
external dependencies are not present in this workspace
(\S\ref{sec:experimental-validation}).
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Reproducibility Statement}

Every table and figure in this report is generated by a checked-in,
deterministically seeded computational pipeline and can be regenerated
exactly. The mathematical claims are backed by executable unit tests, not
asserted: the tests verify the energy-backtracking safeguard and the $M_{ij}$
construction (symmetry, positive semidefiniteness, exact agreement with the
closed-form recursion), the $L_1$-kinetic sign bound (Lion), the
decoupled-decay/$L_2$ distinction (AdamW), the $\operatorname{vec}(AXB)$
Kronecker identity (Shampoo), and the spectral-entropy maximality of
orthogonalization (Muon). The benchmark pipeline writes its CSV/PNG outputs
under \texttt{visualizations/} and prints the exact numbers quoted in this
report; all code is NumPy-only with fixed, documented random seeds.

\section{Conclusion}

The analysis supports three conclusions. First, the Hamiltonian--geometric
framework is mathematically coherent once the spectral-force sign convention
and memory-metric construction are made explicit. The Legendre transform,
Hamilton equations, flat-metric reduction, optimizer special cases,
variational Adam derivation, memory recursion, and spectral-entropy gradient
all admit direct numerical verification. The symmetry checks further confirm
that metric symmetrization and spectral entropy preserve the invariances
required by the Hamiltonian formulation.

Second, the empirical results indicate that the method is strongest when the
loss contains exploitable full-metric structure. This is most visible in the
ill-conditioned rotated quadratic and in the phase-space diagnostics, where a
coupled metric removes coordinate-conditioning effects that diagonal and flat
methods cannot represent. The controlled ablation study reinforces this
interpretation: under an unrelated metric, the correction terms are nearly
inert, whereas under the Hessian metric the spectral term has a large and
statistically significant effect ($p=3.2\times10^{-8}$). The same ablation
also shows that the correction terms act as a stability unit; partial
combinations can introduce rare divergent trajectories that are not captured
by average-case summaries alone.

Third, the benchmark evidence should be interpreted as competitive rather
than universally dominant. Hamiltonian-geometric performs strongly on the
classical tables and the kicked-top control instance, and it ties heavy-ball
on QAOA MaxCut. Against modern optimizers derived as additional members of
the same family -- AdamW, Lion, Shampoo, and Muon -- it lies in the leading
group but does not separate decisively from Adam or Muon. The most defensible
claim is therefore not that Hamiltonian-geometric supersedes existing
optimizers, but that it provides a coherent unifying geometry in which many
optimizer families arise from specific choices of metric, kinetic term, and
potential splitting, while remaining empirically competitive on the benchmark
suite studied here.

Fourth, two follow-up studies (\S\ref{sec:robustness-followup}) were run
specifically to stress-test rather than confirm this picture, since a report
that only ever finds favorable results warrants suspicion. The near-tie with
Adam and Muon survives a 10-seed check, but a hidden-layer-width sweep with
the hyperparameters held fixed shows Adam's validation loss improving
monotonically with width while Hamiltonian-geometric's does not, overtaking
it by width 96 -- direct evidence that the tuned advantage does not
extrapolate to larger models without re-tuning. Separately, the memory-metric
completion $M_{ij}$ (\S\ref{sec:memory-metric}), while mathematically
well-posed and unit-verified, measurably \emph{hurt} performance on the one
real benchmark it was tested against and is not claimed as a practical
improvement. Both results are included because the honest boundary of a
method matters as much as its favorable cases.
