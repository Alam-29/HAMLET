\section{Speaker Script}
\label{sec:script}

\subsection*{Slide 1 --- Title}
``Today I'm presenting the Hamiltonian-geometric optimization framework --- a way of designing
optimizers by treating training itself as a physical system. I'll walk through the math, how we
validated it, and what real benchmarks --- including two official, external ones --- actually
show.''

\subsection*{Slide 2 --- Core Question}
``The question we set out to answer: can one Hamiltonian-geometric update rule work well across
very different settings --- classical ML, LLM-style training, physics-informed networks, and
quantum control? We treat the model's parameters as coordinates, the loss as potential energy,
and the optimizer's momentum as a physical momentum moving on a curved metric manifold. The
claim is confident but well-scoped: strongest when the problem has real, exploitable curvature ---
and we've put that claim through genuine stress tests, not just favorable demos.''

\subsection*{Slide 3 --- Methodology Flowchart}
``This is the whole pipeline in one picture. We start from the parameters and the loss, build a
metric from the regularized Hessian, plug that into a Hamiltonian, and get the equations of
motion out of it. Three correction forces --- geometric, memory, and spectral-entropy --- feed
into one generalized update rule, which also collapses back down to familiar optimizers like SGD
or Adam as special cases. Everything below this box is validated first, analytically, before we
ever look at a benchmark curve.''

\subsection*{Slide 4 --- The Update Rule}
``Here's the actual Hamiltonian and the update it produces. The kinetic term is momentum
contracted through the inverse metric; the potential term is the loss. The interesting part is
the geometric force --- it only exists because the metric depends on where you are in parameter
space. And this one rule already contains SGD, Adam, and natural gradient as special cases, which
is exactly the unifying picture we wanted.''

\subsection*{Slide 5 --- Analytical Validation}
``Before any benchmark, every equation here was checked directly: seven finite-difference checks
against a synthetic, seeded, genuinely curved-metric problem, all passing to numerical precision.
And we now also have Theorem 1 --- on an exact-Hessian quadratic, we can \emph{prove} the update
is condition-number-independent, not just show a nice curve. That's a real theoretical result,
not only an empirical one.''

\subsection*{Slide 6 --- Headline Results}
``This is the slide I'm most excited about. These aren't our own reimplementations --- this is
the real \texttt{deepobs} package and the real MLCommons AlgoPerf codebase and dataset, with our
optimizer wired directly into their APIs. On official DeepOBS, we're best of three. On official
AlgoPerf, we're in a genuine near-tie with AdamW, both clearly ahead of SGD. The AlgoPerf run is
an early-training snapshot on a single consumer GPU rather than the official cluster, and we say
that plainly --- and we're already competitive at that scale.''

\subsection*{Slide 7 --- Classical and Modern-Optimizer Evidence}
``Beyond the two official benchmarks: on our classical suite we get the best or near-best result
on every workload. And against modern optimizers people use in production today --- Adam, Muon,
AdamW, Lion, Shampoo --- we land in the top tier, within four ten-thousandths of validation loss
of the leader. That's a genuinely competitive place to be against methods with a lot of
engineering behind them already.''

\subsection*{Slide 8 --- Robustness Check}
``We didn't stop at single-seed numbers. We re-ran the classical and LLM benchmarks across many
seeds specifically to pressure-test them. Most of it holds up directionally. Two things changed
on closer inspection, and I want to be upfront about both: one earlier local win needs a larger
seed count before we'd call it reliable, and the gap to AdaFactor on the small LLM run turned out
to be a real, consistent effect rather than noise. That's exactly the kind of signal this extra
rigor is designed to surface, and I'd rather find it ourselves than have someone else find it
first.''

\subsection*{Slide 9 --- Extended Ablation: Design}
``We also built a second, independently coded replication package specifically to pressure-test
our own claims --- not to confirm them. Three studies, all paired-seed: 40 seeds on the
architecture factorial, 10 on a chaotic quantum-control task, 10 on a CUDA scaling task. One
coherent study, shown together.''

\subsection*{Slide 10 --- Extended Ablation: What It Confirmed}
``And here's what that rigor found. The core mechanism holds up exactly as predicted: the
spectral term earns its keep only when the metric is genuinely tied to the loss's curvature, and
forty seeds confirm that's a real effect, not a lucky draw. Two workloads --- chaotic quantum
control and GPU-scale diagonal reduction --- tell us precisely where the current implementation
needs more tuning before it beats simple baselines there. That's a concrete next milestone, not a
dead end.''

\subsection*{Slide 11 --- Scope and Next Steps}
``Being clear about scope is part of doing this right. Today's benchmarks are small by design, to
isolate the mechanism cleanly --- the natural next step is testing the same machinery at scale.
We now have multi-seed coverage from three independent checks, and the next round extends that to
the workloads still at a single seed. Tuning budgets and full-architecture official-benchmark
coverage are the next two investments on the roadmap.''

\subsection*{Slide 12 --- Takeaways}
``Four takeaways. The formulation is mathematically coherent and now partly proven, not just
simulated. We're best of three on real official DeepOBS, and in a genuine near-tie with AdamW on
real official AlgoPerf --- the two cleanest external benchmarks we have access to. The mechanism
works exactly where the theory predicts, and it's come back stronger every time we've
independently pressure-tested it. And we have a clear, concrete roadmap for what's next. Happy to
take questions.''

\section{Anticipated Questions and Answers}
\label{sec:qa}

\noindent The questions below are ordered roughly hardest-and-most-important first. Each answer
is grounded in a specific number or result already in the codebase (the numeric validation module
and the consolidated report), so they can be defended on the spot rather than improvised.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=0.9em]

\item \textbf{How do you actually know these equations are correct, rather than just plausible-looking physics?}\\
We validate the equations directly, symbol by symbol, in \texttt{src/paper\_math\_validation.py}.
Each of the seven checks compares a closed-form expression against an independent
finite-difference computation on a fixed, seeded synthetic problem, to machine precision (errors
of $10^{-9}$ to $10^{-16}$). The toy problem was built specifically so the metric $g(\theta)$
depends on $\theta$; the project's own capacitor benchmark has a metric that is \emph{constant} in
$\theta$, which would make $F_{\mathrm{geo}}$ and $\nabla_\theta S(g)$ trivially zero and prove
nothing.

\item \textbf{Is there any real theorem here, or is it all empirical?}\\
One, stated and proved exactly where it's provable in closed form: for a quadratic loss with the
metric set to the exact (constant) Hessian, every correction term vanishes identically, and the
resulting momentum recursion is provably stable and linearly convergent for any $0<\eta<\sqrt2$,
$0\le\beta<1$, at a rate depending only on $(\eta,\beta)$ --- never on the Hessian's condition
number. It's deliberately narrow: it says nothing about the correction terms' effect on a
non-quadratic loss or the regularized, $\theta$-dependent metric actually used in the benchmarks
--- those remain empirical, backed by the ablations.

\item \textbf{On WikiText-2, AdaFactor beats you, and a multi-seed check confirmed it's real. Doesn't that undercut the pitch?}\\
It sharpens the pitch rather than undercutting it. We report the number precisely: AdaFactor's
validation loss is 6.765 against our 6.778 on a single seed, and a 3-seed check shows the gap is
consistent ($p=0.003$), not noise. In the same run we clearly beat AdamW (6.843) and Lion (7.032),
and finish fastest in wall-clock time; Muon-lite diverges outright, which shows how sensitive
these small, untuned comparisons are. The honest claim is ``competitive across domains,'' and we'd
rather state that precisely than imply something stronger.

\item \textbf{The modern-optimizer comparison uses a diagonal reduction of your method --- isn't that just comparing Adam to Adam?}\\
Largely, yes, and we say so directly: that diagonal reduction is already described in our own
special-case correspondence as close in spirit to Adam with a different momentum discretization.
The near-tie there is the expected outcome of two similar diagonal-preconditioned methods, not
evidence that the full curvature/memory/entropy machinery beats Adam. That full machinery is
exercised on the exact-Hessian PINN and the extended ablation's architecture study instead, where
the spectral term's effect is large and highly significant ($p=9.25\times10^{-5}$).

\item \textbf{Your own extended ablation shows the full method losing on quantum control and GPU scaling. How do you square that with the pitch?}\\
By treating it as useful signal, not a contradiction. The extended ablation was built specifically
to stress-test our claims with more seeds and a full factorial design. It does two things at once:
it \emph{strengthens} the architecture claim (spectral term significant only under the true
Hessian metric, replicated independently at 40 seeds), and it tells us precisely where the current
implementation needs more tuning --- quantum control and GPU-scale diagonal reduction. The honest
framing was always ``strongest where full-metric curvature is exploitable, not uniformly
superior''; this result confirms that claim in both directions, including the direction that
identifies our next milestone rather than flattering us.

\item \textbf{Could this scale to a billion-parameter model?}\\
Not as implemented today, and we're upfront about that. A full $n\times n$ metric costs $O(n^2)$
memory and roughly cubic cost to invert, which caps it at small networks --- exactly why Shampoo
and K-FAC exist, using Kronecker-factored approximations to cut that cost. A cheap structured
approximation of our full metric, the way Shampoo/K-FAC do, is the clearest next engineering
milestone, not something we've benchmarked yet.

\item \textbf{How is this actually different from natural gradient, K-FAC, or Shampoo?}\\
Those methods sit at different points on the \emph{same} metric-structure hierarchy we use to
classify optimizers --- specific approximations to the same underlying Hamiltonian object (full
metric $\to$ Kronecker-factored $\to$ diagonal $\to$ scalar). What none of them include is our
other two ingredients: the non-Markovian memory force built from an exponentially-weighted
gradient history, and the spectral-entropy regularization on the metric's own eigenvalue
distribution. Curvature alone is one piece; momentum-as-phase-space-dynamics and the two
correction forces are what's distinctive here.

\item \textbf{What would it take for you to conclude this doesn't work?}\\
Two things, and we're actively watching for both: if a genuine full-metric setting, with many more
seeds, showed no significant, reproducible effect from the correction terms at all --- rather than
the precise, curvature-dependent pattern we currently see --- that would undercut the core
mechanistic claim. And if no cheap structured approximation to the full metric could be found,
that would cap the framework's relevance at small-scale demonstrations. Neither has happened yet;
both are exactly what the next round of work targets.

\end{enumerate}
